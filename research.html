A deeper look at the history, mechanics, and culture surrounding the guillotine.

1. The Paradox of "Humane" Design

The Enlightenment Origin

Paradoxically, the guillotine was born from a desire for mercy. Dr. Joseph-Ignace Guillotin, a physician, proposed it as a way to end the "barbaric" and often botched executions of the Ancien Régime (breaking on the wheel, hanging, or clumsy sword beheadings).

The Great Equalizer

Before the Revolution, decapitation was a "privilege" reserved for nobles. Dr. Guillotin's law made it the sole method of execution, ensuring that in death, the peasant and the king were treated exactly the same.

AR Narrative Hook

You could focus on the "clinical efficiency" of the device. Users could see the transition from chaotic, manual execution tools (swords, axes. etc…) to the cold, mathematical precision of the machine.

2. Technical Anatomy & UX of the Machine

The "Lunette"

The hinged wooden collar that locked the victim's neck in place.

The Blade

Originally straight or crescent-shaped, King Louis XVI (who would later die by the device) reportedly suggested the oblique (angled) blade to ensure it would slice through any neck regardless of anatomy.

Speed of Operation

At the height of the "Reign of Terror," executioners like Charles-Henri Sanson could process a victim every two to three minutes. The sound like the "whish" of the blade became instantly recognizable among French citizens.

AR Narrative Hook

An AR "X-ray" view of the machine's internal mechanics (the pulley, the weighted "mouton," and the release latch) could show how engineering was used to industrialize death.

3. The "Sanson" Dynasty: The Human Element

The Family Curse

The Sansons were a multi-generational dynasty of executioners. They were social outcasts, they were forbidden from marrying outside executioner families and often forced to wear distinctive clothing, yet they were "celebrities" of the scaffold.

The Reluctant Headsman

Charles-Henri Sanson, the most famous, was a man of high culture who played the violin and studied medicine. He famously executed Louis XVI, a man he had previously served as a royal official.

AR Narrative Hook

Using letters or journals from the Sanson family to provide a "first-person" perspective of the person pulling the lever.

4. Cultural "Guillotine-Mania"

Public Spectacle

Executions were not private; they were festivals. Vendors sold "souvenirs," programs with the names of the condemned, and even food at the "Cabaret de la Guillotine."

Children's Toys

During the 1790s, two-foot-tall toy guillotines were popular among children, who used them to decapitate dolls or even small rodents. Women even wore small, guillotine-shaped earrings in solidarity.
